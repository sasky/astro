To me, NZ music can loosely be grouped into three eras, those being:

## The Nature's Best Era

In January of 2002 a double compilation CD came out called 'Nature's Best'. This CD shortly after became a fixture in every suburban middle-class home around the Motu. The CD reminded us that NZ has made some really great music over the decades, and in fact we should be proud of it. It was the spark that led to the birth of the music renaissance (see below).
Now the music in question was mainly your classic boomer grooves of yesteryear. Your Crowded Houses, Dave Dobbyn, Exponents, Split Enz and the like. Real bangers which I dare say are now more on the overplayed end of the cultural mainstream.
But for the time the music was a wave of euphoria that swept the nation, much like finding your favourite pair of socks under the fridge years after one had given up all hope.

## The '90s Era

The Nature's Best CDs did have a few '90s tracks on them, but they kind of felt a bit token, not really complementing the more boomer musical journey. The exception to this rule was of course Bic Runga's 'Sway', which was an immediate hit with all generations. Permatly cementing a young Bic Runga as one of our cultural treasures, right up there with Dame Kiri and Sir Howard.

The larger '90s era was all about young rock of various flavours. We're talking your Shihad, Headless Chickens, Garageland, Head Like a Hole, Supergroove and the Feelers coming up the rear.
'90s bands need extra props for finding a local audience in a decade of more broader cultural cringe. Mainstream TV and radio did not support local acts. Great music was being made in spite of cultural indifference.

## The Renaissance Era

One Mike Chunn of Split Enz fame, who spearheaded the Nature's Best CDs (see above), was also instrumental in kicking off NZ Music Week (and later Month) to celebrate and peer-pressure TV and radio into playing more NZ music. Over the late '90s and early 2000s, NZ Music Month was overwhelmingly successful. Not only at getting more local play on the radio, but also at turning the tide with the public at large, turning once ye-rolling sceptics into fully signed-up, card-carrying local music enthusiasts . This, combined with the rise of music festivals post the mud-ridden hippy love-fest success of the Gathering, saw NZ music explode into a plethora of genres and sub-genres, remaining strong ever since .
