To me, NZ music can loosely be grouped into four eras, those being, which somehow line up quite nicely with the stages of my life. I am indeed fortunate that my lived experience lines up perfectly with the objective cultural pulse of the nation.

### The Nature's Best Era

<YouTube id="JB2EiHOB0Mw" />

In January of 2002 a double compilation CD came out called ['Nature's Best'](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nature%27s_Best) This CD shortly after became a fixture in all respectable suburban middle-class homes around the Motu. The CD
unapologetically shone the spotlight on our back catalog, once tucked away dusty on the shelf, brushed off re discovered as treasure hiding in plain sight.
t was the spark that led to the birth of the music renaissance (see below).
Now the music in question was mainly your classic boomer grooves of yesteryear. Your Crowded Houses, Dave Dobbyn, Exponents, Split Enz and the like. Real bangers which I dare say are now more on the overplayed end of the cultural mainstream.
Much like finding your favourite pair of socks under the fridge years after one had given up all hope, we put the Natures Best socks back on, and the fit was still great.

### The 90s Era

<YouTube id="BZ2ZkM1kBo4" />

The Nature's Best CDs did have a few '90s tracks on them, but they kind of felt a bit token, not really complementing the more boomer pop-rock musical journey. The exception to this rule was of course Bic Runga's 'Sway', which was an immediate hit with all generations. Permanently cementing a young Bic Runga as one of our cultural treasures, right up there with Dame Kiri and Sir Howard.
To my memory, the larger '90s era was all about young rock of various flavours. Bands such as Shihad, Headless Chickens, Garageland, Head Like a Hole, Supergroove and the Feelers coming up the rear.
90s bands need extra props for finding a local audience in a decade of more broader cultural cringe. Mainstream TV and radio did not support local acts. Great music was being made in spite of a larger cultural indifference outside of uni circles
(at this point I'm just making shit up, what do I know? I was 8-18 in the 90's, zits of various shapes and sizes were of more pressing concern than keeping tap of the cultural pulse of my elders )

### The Renaissance Era

One Mike Chunn of Split Enz fame, who spearheaded the Nature's Best CDs (see above), was also instrumental in kicking off NZ Music Week (and later Month) to celebrate and peer-pressure TV and radio into playing more NZ music. Over the late '90s and early 2000s, NZ Music Month was overwhelmingly successful. Not only at getting more local play on the radio, but also at turning the tide with the public at large, turning once eye-rolling sceptics into fully signed-up, card-carrying local music enthusiasts. This, combined with the rise of music festivals post the mud-ridden hippy love-fest success of the Gathering, saw NZ music explode into a plethora of genres and sub-genres, remaining strong ever since .

### The Contemporary Era

The Contemporary era kicks off 2010ish, which aligns nicely with me hitting my thirties while simultaneously moving to the cultural Groundhog Day vortex of Queenstown. To the more obtuse reader would have observed that, much like belly button fluff circles around the plug hole, I have been circling round the idea of personal life experience equalling objective national cultural trends. I am self enlightened enough to appreciate such hubris. Indeed, it is the natural order of things to believe the 'best' music was the music discovered in ones youth. and all music floating past the age of 30 is somehow, just not quite as good.
An objectionable unprovable hypnosis of course. However one tends to reach for nostalgia over scientific curiosity when selecting bangers for the more mature punters at the house party.
The Contemporary era is usually discovered by yours truly having one to many cab sav's on a Saturday night at home, digging through latest offerings on Spotify in a desperate attempt to dig for the fountain of youth.
Indeed there is is gold to be found here, and it needs to shine evermore brightly to penetrate though the instincts of the elderly to open up ones heart to the new while simultaneously resisting the strong pull to instead revisit the old and familiar.
